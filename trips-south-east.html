England at its Best

Route

Today’s trip will take us over a number of iconic sites in the South East of England. We will overﬂy castles from the medieval to the Victorian, marvels of architecture and engineering, centres of great cultural importance and World Heritage Sites. We will see some of the most important and beloved symbols of our civilisation from the past five thousand years. Starting in Buckinghamshire, we will be completing a clockwise loop that will include a journey above the famed River Thames and get the chance to see London in a way that most of its 15+ million annual visitors could only dream of. A view of Sir Winston Churchill’s home will be followed by a number of castles and the worldrenowned White Cliffs of Dover. A break in a quaint East Sussex pub will be followed by a ﬂight over the famous south coast town of Brighton and the Isle of Wight and lunch at a 5 star luxury country house hotel in Hampshire. After lunch we will move on to the ancient sites of Stonehenge and Avebury before tea with Sir Michael. We will end our day back at HQ Aviation in Denham.

Destination Guide

Windsor Castle

Windsor Castle, the largest and oldest occupied castle in the world, is one of the official residences of Her Majesty The Queen. The Castle's dramatic site encapsulates 900 years of British history. It covers an area of 26 acres and contains (as well as a royal palace) a magnificent chapel and the homes and workplaces of a large number of people.

Hampton Court Palace

Hampton Court Palace is a royal palace in the London Borough of Richmond upon Thames in the historic county of Middlesex. Originally built in 1514 for Cardinal Wolsey it is one of the only two surviving palaces once owned and lived in by Henry VIII.

River Thames - The Oval

The Oval is an international cricket ground at Kennington. It has been the home ground of Surrey County Cricket Club since it was built in 1845. It was the first ground in England to host international Test Cricket, in September 1880. The final Test match of the English season is traditionally played there. It has also hosted various other sporting occasions including football and rugby cup finals and can lay claim to being the most historically important general sports ground in the world.

River Thames - Westminster Abbey

Westminster Abbey is a large Gothic cathedral in the City of Westminster near the Palace of Westminster. It is one of the most notable religious buildings in the United Kingdom and has been the traditional place of coronation and burial site for British monarchs. There have been at least 16 royal weddings at the abbey, including the Duke and Dutchess of Cambridge.

River Thames - Big Ben

Big Ben, the nickname for the Great Bell of the clock, and the Houses of Parliament are at the north end of the Palace of Westminster in London. The tower has become one of the most prominent symbols of the United Kingdom and is often in the establishing shot of films set in London.

River Thames - The London Eye

The London Eye, opened in 2000 is the world’s tallest cantilevered observation wheel, found on the South Bank of the River Thames in London and also known as the Millennium Wheel. It offered the highest public viewing point in London until it was superseded by the observation deck on the 72nd ﬂoor of The Shard.

River Thames - Trafalgar Square

Trafalgar Square is a public space and tourist attraction in central London. At its centre is Nelson's Column, which is guarded by four lion statues at its base. There are a number of commemorative statues and sculptures in the square, while one plinth, left empty since it was built in 1840, The Fourth Plinth, has been host to contemporary art since 1999.

River Thames - Millenium Bridge and St. Paul's Cathedral

The Millennium Bridge is a steel suspension bridge for pedestrians crossing the River Thames in London - the first for more than a century. St Paul's is an iconic feature of the London skyline. The cathedral is one of the most famous and most recognisable sights of London, with its dome, framed by the spires of Wren's City churches, dominating the skyline for 300 years.

Rive Thames - The Shard and the Gherkin

The vision for the shard was to create an architecturally striking vertical city incorporating retail, offices, a hotel, apartments and a public viewing gallery. Standing at almost 310 metres, the shard is one of the tallest buildings in Western Europe. The Gherkin is a 41 storey, 180 metre tall commercial skyscraper that has become one of the city's most widely recognised examples of contemporary architecture.

River Thames - Tower of London

Her Majesty's Royal Palace and Fortress is a historic castle located on the north bank of the River Thames. The castle was used as a prison from 1100 until 1952, although that was not its primary purpose. A grand palace early in its history, it served as a royal residence, and has a dark reputation as the place high-profile prisoners were held before execution, including Anne Boleyn and Guy Fawkes. As a whole, the Tower is a complex of several buildings set within two concentric rings of defensive walls and a moat.

River Thames - Greenwich Park

Greenwich Park is one of the capital's eight Royal Parks and hosts the Prime Meridian Line and Royal Observatory as well as being part of the Greenwich Maritime World Heritage Site which is home to The National Maritime Museum and Old Royal Naval College. The most historic of all Royal Parks, Greenwich Park dates back to Roman times and was enclosed in 1427.

Chartwell

Winston Churchill's family home from 1922 which is currently under the administration of the National Trust. In 1946 the Churchills could no longer afford to keep the property, which was then bought by wealthy businessmen. The arrangement involved payment of nominal rent by both Sir Winston and Lady Churchill, which gave them the right to live there until they died, at which point the property was presented to the National Trust.

Hever Castle

The Hever Castle, originally a country house, is a historic 13th century castle in Kent. From 1462 to 1539 it was the seat of the Boleyn family (Anne Boleyn, the second queen consort of King Henry VIII of England, spent her early youth there). It is double-moated and has 125 acres of spectacular garden, including a yew maze. In summer the castle still plays host to jousting tournaments.

Sissinghurst Castle

Vita Sackville-West and Sir Harold Nicolson, part of the Bloomsbury set, created this internationally renowned garden in the 1930s. They rescued and restored the derelict 14th century Elizabethan manor house, surrounded by farmland and orchards.

Leeds Castle

Leeds Castle is a popular tourist attraction in Kent, built almost 900 years ago in 1119. A residence of Edward I, and later Catherine of Aragon, this twelfth century stronghold has since served as a royal palace, a prison and as a stately home.

White Cliffs of Dover

Forming part of the English coastline facing the Strait of Dover and France, the White Cliffs of Dover are part of the North Downs formation. The cliffs spread east and west from the town of Dover in the county of Kent, an ancient and still important English port. It marks the point where Great Britain most closely approaches continental Europe. On a clear day, the cliffs are easily visible from the French coast.

The Channel Tunnel

The Channel Tunnel links Folkestone in the United Kingdom with Coquelles near Calais in northern France, beneath the English Channel at the Strait of Dover. The tunnel has the longest undersea portion of any tunnel in the world.

Bodiam Castle

Bodiam Castle is a 14th-century moated castle near Robertsbridge in East Sussex. It was built in 1385 by Sir Edward Dalyngrigge, a former knight of Edward III, with the permission of Richard II, ostensibly to defend the area against French invasion during the Hundred Years' War.

Battle Abbey

Battle Abbey is a partially ruined abbey complex in the small town of Battle in East Sussex, England. The abbey was built on the scene of the Battle of Hastings and dedicated to St. Martin. Therefore, it is one of the most historically important Historic Sites in England.

Brighton Pier

Brighton pier, formally known as The Brighton Marine Palace and Pier, is now Brighton’s only non-derelict pier and has since become a major tourist destination.

Solent Forts

No Man's Land Fort is a fort 2.2 kilometres off the coast of the Isle of Wight. Spitbank Fort, also known as Spitsand Fort or Spit Fort, is a sea fort built as a result of the 1859 Royal Commission. The forts are two of four located in the Solent, near Portsmouth, England.

Longford Castle

Longford Castle was built by Sir Thomas Gorges in the 14th Century with an unusual triangular design. The gardens have since been restored and new formal gardens have been created.

Stonehenge

Stonehenge is a prehistoric monument in Wiltshire, England. It was built around 3100BC taking about 30 million man-hours. The remains are of a ring of standing stones set within earthworks. It is in the middle of the most dense complex of Neolithic and Bronze Age monuments in England, including several hundred burial mounds.

Avebury Ring

Avebury is a Neolithic henge monument containing three stone circles, around the village of Avebury in Wiltshire, in southwest England. Unique amongst megalithic monuments, Avebury contains the largest stone circle in Europe, and is one of the best known prehistoric sites in Britain. It is both a tourist attraction and a place of religious importance to contemporary Pagans.

Blenheim Palace

Blenheim Palace is a monumental country house situated in Woodstock. Home to the 11th Duke and Duchess of Marlborough and birthplace of Sir Winston Churchill, Blenheim Palace is a true masterpiece of 18th Century Baroque architecture. This World Heritage Site is set amongst 2000 acres of parkland and Formal Gardens.

University of Oxford

The University of Oxford is a world-famous collegiate research university in Oxford. After disputes between students and Oxford townsfolk in 1209, some academics ﬂed northeast to Cambridge, where they established what became the University of Cambridge. The two "ancient universities" are frequently jointly referred to as “Oxbridge”.

Waddesdon Manor

Waddesdon Manor is a country house managed by the National Trust in Buckinghamshire set in formal gardens and an English landscape park. The house was built in the NeoRenaissance style of a French château between 1874 and 1889 for Baron Ferdinand de Rothschild. Several television series have been shot at Waddesdon Manor including ‘The Queen’ and scenes of ‘Downton Abbey.’

HQ Aviation - Denham

A world-class aviation facility with everything a helicopter pilot - past, present, or future - could possibly want or need.
